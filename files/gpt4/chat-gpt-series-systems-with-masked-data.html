Expectation-Maximization (EM) and Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) are both statistical methods used to estimate the parameters of a model. While they share similarities, they have some key differences in their applications and assumptions:

The EM algorithm consists of two steps: the Expectation (E) step, where the missing data is estimated given the current parameter estimates, and the Maximization (M) step, where the parameters are updated based on the complete data, including the estimated missing values from the E step.
  • MLE: MLE is a general method for estimating parameters of a distribution based on observed data. MLE does not inherently handle incomplete data, and additional adjustments (such as censoring) must be made to the likelihood function to account for the missing values.
  1. Convergence:
  • EM: The EM algorithm converges to a local maximum of the likelihood function. This means that the algorithm might not find the true global maximum, and the final estimates may depend on the initial values of the parameters.
  • MLE: In many cases, MLE converges to the global maximum of the likelihood function, given that certain regularity conditions are met. However, finding the global maximum might be computationally challenging, especially for complex models with many parameters.
  1. Ease of implementation:
  • EM: Implementing the EM algorithm might be more involved, as it requires defining the E and M steps and iterating between them until convergence. The difficulty of implementation depends on the complexity of the model and the specific form of the E and M steps.
  • MLE: MLE typically involves finding the parameters that maximize the likelihood function, which might be relatively straightforward for simple models. However, for more complex models, the optimization problem might require advanced techniques or numerical methods.
  1. Assumptions:
  • EM: The EM algorithm assumes that the missing data is missing at random (MAR), meaning that the probability of missingness depends only on the observed data and not on the missing data itself.
  • MLE: MLE does not make explicit assumptions about the missing data mechanism. However, when dealing with incomplete data, additional assumptions might be needed to adjust the likelihood function properly.

In summary, the EM algorithm is more suitable for situations with incomplete data or latent variables, while MLE is a general method for parameter estimation.
