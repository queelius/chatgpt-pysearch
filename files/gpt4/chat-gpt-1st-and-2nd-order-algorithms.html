What does a first-order algorithm mean? Using the local gradient information? Second-order? Hessian?

A first-order algorithm refers to an optimization method that uses local gradient information (i.e., first-order derivatives) to update the parameters of a model, function, or system. Gradient Descent and its variants (such as Stochastic Gradient Descent and Adam) are popular first-order algorithms used in machine learning and deep learning for optimizing loss functions.

A second-order algorithm, on the other hand, uses not only the gradient information but also the second-order derivatives, specifically the Hessian matrix. The Hessian is a square matrix that represents the curvature of the function in the neighborhood of a point. Second-order algorithms, such as Newton's method, use both the gradient and the Hessian to find more accurate and efficient updates to the parameters.

In summary:

  1. First-order algorithm: Uses local gradient information (first-order derivatives) to update parameters. Example: Gradient Descent.
  2. Second-order algorithm: Uses local gradient information and second-order derivatives (Hessian matrix) to update parameters. Example: Newton's method.
What's a zeroth-order?

A zeroth-order optimization algorithm is a method that uses only function evaluations (i.e., the output of the function itself) to update the parameters of a model, function, or system, without requiring any gradient (first-order) or Hessian (second-order) information. These algorithms are particularly useful when the gradient information is not available or is computationally expensive to calculate.

One common example of a zeroth-order optimization method is Random Search, where the algorithm tries random combinations of parameter values within a specified range to find the optimal solution. Another example is the Nelder-Mead method (also known as the simplex method), which uses a simplex (a polytope in n-dimensional space) to find the optimal solution by iteratively reflecting, expanding, and contracting the simplex vertices.

Zeroth-order optimization methods can be less efficient and slower to converge compared to first- and second-order methods when dealing with high-dimensional problems or smooth functions, as they do not utilize gradient information to guide the search. However, they can be useful in cases where the objective function is non-differentiable, noisy, or has many local optima.

What's a fractional order algorithm?

A fractional-order algorithm is an optimization or control method that involves the use of fractional-order calculus, which is an extension of traditional integer-order calculus. Fractional-order calculus deals with derivatives and integrals of non-integer orders, allowing for more complex and flexible mathematical models.

Fractional-order algorithms can be applied to various fields, including optimization, control systems, and signal processing. They are particularly useful in situations where traditional integer-order methods may not adequately capture the dynamics of a system or process, such as systems with memory or hereditary properties.

In the context of optimization, fractional-order algorithms are relatively less explored compared to zeroth-, first-, and second-order methods. However, researchers have started investigating the potential of fractional-order calculus to improve optimization techniques, such as gradient-based methods and swarm intelligence algorithms.

It is important to note that fractional-order algorithms are not as widespread or well-established as their integer-order counterparts, and their application and performance may vary depending on the specific problem being addressed.
